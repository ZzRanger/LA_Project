Overview

Twain's Message

The great American classic The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn is widely interpreted as a satire of Southern society. A critical component of this satire in Mark Twain's novel is the hypocritical behavior many characters in the book display.

There are many instances of various characters in the book acting hypocritically (i.e. Widow, Pap, King & Duke, Grangerfords & Sheperdsons). Twain did this primarily to criticize the norms and beliefs held by those in Southern society.

The instances of hypocrisy in the book can be broken down into three main categories: racial hypocrisy, moral hypocrisy, and religious hypocrisy.

Racial Hypocrisy

Exemplified by the King and the Duke

During the middle of the book when the Duke and King came to a town posing to be Peter Wilks' brothers from England, the Duke and King secured the $6,000 inheritance Peter Wilks left for his brother. Later, when the inheritance was stolen by Huck, the King and the Duke blamed it on the African American slaves that took care of the house they lived in. The irony here is that the two men are the true thieves of the inheritance. This example speaks highlights the unfair prejudice many whites in Southern society held.

"Where is [the money], then?"

"Well, when my niece give it to me to keep for her I took and hid it inside o' the straw tick o' my bed, not wishin' to bank it... The [slaves] stole it the very next mornin' after I had went down-stairs; and when I sold 'em I hadn't missed the money yit, so they got clean away with it." (Twain 198)

Religious Hypocrisy

"Next Sunday we all went to church, about three mile, everybody a-horseback. The men took their guns along, so did Buck, and kept them between their knees...it was pretty ornery preaching---all about brotherly love...everyone said it was a good sermon, and they all talked it over going home..." (Twain 109)

In the middle of the book, Huck is taken in by the Grangerfords, a wealthy Southern aristocratic family. He quickly learns about their violent feud with the Sheperdsons, another wealthy Southern family that live in the same town. When Sunday rolls around, both families attend church, where the pastor is giving a sermon on brotherly love, which the congregation had a positive reaction to. Ironically, both families brought their guns to church and kept them between their laps throughout the whole sermon. Twain included this scene in the book to criticize those in Southern society who claim to be religious but disregard many of its teachings to fit their on worldview.

Moral Hypocrisy

Widow Douglas

Huck's "Guardian"
"Pretty soon I wanted to smoke, and asked the widow to let me. But she wouldn't. She said it was a mean practice and wasn't clean, andI must try to not do it any more...and she took snuff, too; of course that was all right, because she done it herself." (Twain 2)
At the beginning of the book, Huck was adopted by the Widow Douglas, who wanted to 'sivilize' him. One day, the Widow got upset at Huck for asking to smoke a cigar, as she saw it as immoral. Ironically, the Widow used tabacco (which arguably has the same stigma as cigars) and said that was okay to use it since she used it herself. This instance of hypocrisy demonstrates the double standards many people in society have regarding ethics and morality. Furthermore, it illustrates a "fear of the unknown" mentality prevalent in Southern society.

Impact of Hypocrisy in the Novel

The characters in the book each represent a certain sterotype in Southern society. By having instances of hypocrisy among many distinct characters in the book, Twain was able to criticize Southern society in totality, particularly condemning systemic raical bias against African Americans, lack of sincerity in upholding religious principles, and double standards for morality.
